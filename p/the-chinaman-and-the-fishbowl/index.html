The chinaman and the fishbowl

June 29, 2026

Watch this video before reading.
​
There's an old Chinese magician in The Prestige named Chung Ling Soo, and the whole film sort of orbits his one trick. He produces a bowl of goldfish out of nowhere, this big heavy glass bowl full of water with live fish in it, appearing from under his robes right in the middle of the act. Angier watches the show and writes him off as a washed up hack who can barely walk and keeps running the same few tricks.

Borden, sitting right next to him, sees the complete opposite. He catches the way the magician shuffles out afterward, hunched over and dragging himself toward his carriage like every step takes everything he has, and that's the moment the whole thing opens up for him. The limp is the trick.

A bowl that size can only be hidden in one place, between his legs, under the robe, gripped there for the entire performance, which means the man is far stronger and steadier than he looks. The only way to keep a single person from ever suspecting that is to never once let himself be seen moving like a capable man. So he doesn't just play the cripple while he's on stage. He plays it constantly, every hour he's out in the world, for years, because the moment anyone catches him moving normally the whole illusion falls apart.

Borden's the only one in the room who really gets what he's looking at, and he says it almost quietly, half to himself. "Total devotion to his art. Utter self-sacrifice." A man who handed over everything ordinary about being alive just to protect one illusion.

And there's nothing underneath it to even expose. No gadget, no trapdoor, no wire. The only secret is that he was willing to give up his entire life to protect the thing, quietly, with nobody watching and nobody clapping. The willingness to pay that is the method. Whatever you see on stage is just the small visible piece of a cost you'll never get to see.

And if you actually want to be one of these people, that's the entire cost of it. Not the version where you switch it on the second there's an audience and go back to being normal the rest of the time. You carry the bowl when the room is empty. You put in the hours nobody will ever know about, with no one watching and no one clapping, until there's no separate version of you left to clock out and go home to. The act stops being something you perform and turns into the actual shape of who you are. That's the part nobody really wants to hear. The thing you're chasing, the one that looks like a method from the outside, was never a method you could pick up and use. It's a price. And you pay it your whole life, mostly in the moments no one is ever around to see.
