The Cross Walk Paradox

November 28, 2024

​

I saw something wild the other day.

I was walking downtown, coffee in hand, when I noticed this perfect little scene playing out at a crosswalk. It was like watching two different species of humans in their natural habitat.

One group stood there, eyes glued to the crosswalk sign, practically hypnotized by this glowing red hand. Street was empty. Like, completely empty. You could've played a full game of chess in the middle of it. But there they were, waiting... and waiting... because a light bulb told them to.

Then there's this other person. They glance left, right, left again – you know, actually using their eyes for what they're meant for – and just... crosses. Simple as that. Safe as that.

And it hit me. This tiny moment captures something so deep about how people move through life.

Look, this isn't about being a rule breaker or some rebel without a cause. I'm talking about something way more interesting: the difference between people who outsource their thinking and people who actually think.

Let me paint you a picture.

Remember when you were a kid learning to cross the street? What did they teach you? "Look left, look right, look left again." Not "stare at the sign until it gives you permission to live." Somewhere along the way, we forgot the actual lesson.

The sign is a tool, not a brain. Just like:

  • That "perfect" time to start a business (spoiler: it doesn't exist)
  • The "right" way to build a career (double spoiler: also doesn't exist)
  • The "safe" path that everyone else is taking (you get the point)

Here's what's really messing with my head about this whole thing:

The people standing still, waiting for permission? They think they're being safe. But they're actually taking the biggest risk of all – the risk of never developing their own judgment.

Think about that for a second.

They're trading their most valuable asset – independent thinking – for the illusion of safety. It's like wearing a helmet to bed. Sure, you're protected, but from what?

But here's where it gets really interesting.

The most successful people I know? They're like master chess players at a crosswalk. They know when to wait and when to walk. They've developed this almost supernatural ability to read situations rather than just following signs.

I call it "situational mastery." And it's rare as hell.

Because developing real judgment? That's hard. It's uncomfortable. It means being wrong sometimes. It means learning from those mistakes. It means thinking when everyone else is following.

The wild thing is, the bigger the decision, the more people look for their equivalent of a crosswalk sign:

  • "What does everyone else think?"
  • "What's the conventional wisdom?"
  • "What's the safe play?"

But by the time the sign says "WALK," the opportunity has usually already crossed the street.

Here's a thought experiment for you:

Imagine if Google waited for the "right time" to launch. If Amazon stuck to just books because that was the "safe play." If Tesla waited for the "green light" from the traditional auto industry.

The crosswalk paradox shows up everywhere once you start looking for it.

And it reveals this brutal truth: The people who wait for perfect conditions end up waiting forever, while the people who learn to read conditions end up leading.

So next time you're at a crosswalk – or really, any decision point in your life – ask yourself: Am I waiting because it's smart, or am I waiting because I'm afraid to trust my own judgment?

Your answer might explain a lot about where you're stuck and where you could be going.

Just something I've been thinking about.
