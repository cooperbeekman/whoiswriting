The Airport Law

December 11, 2024

I was at Montreal airport Monday afternoon, exactly one hour before my flight to NYC. The amount of people who think this is crazy never fails to amaze me. There's always someone ready to lecture you about security times, traffic possibilities, and the thousand things that could go wrong.

Yet I made my flight. Just like I always do.

What fascinates me about airports is how clearly they expose a fundamental difference in how people think about and assess risk. You've got the majority - the 3-hour-early crowd - living in perpetual anxiety about what could go wrong, and then you've got the calculated players who've taken the time to understand exactly how the game works.

The 3-hour-early crowd thinks they're being smart, responsible, and risk-averse. In reality, they're burning their most precious resource – time – to protect against risks that are largely imaginary. They're paying a premium in life hours for a feeling of safety that could be achieved with actual knowledge instead of arbitrary buffers.

This pattern of unnecessary buffering infects every area of life. People stay in dead-end jobs years too long because they're buffering against the risk of change. They hold onto dying businesses quarters too long because they're buffering against the risk of failure. They sit on revolutionary ideas months too long because they're buffering against the risk of imperfect timing. They're all showing up three hours early to a flight that only needs sixty minutes.

True high performers understand risk differently. They don't pile on security blankets of time and resources - they make precise calculations. When I show up an hour before a flight, it's not because I'm reckless or enjoy cutting things close. It's because I've done the actual math. I know exactly how long security takes at different times of day. I understand the real buffer needed for unexpected events. I've priced in the actual risk and calculated the true cost of failure.

The difference between an hour and three hours at the airport might seem trivial in isolation. But this same pattern of over-buffering against imaginary risks plays out across every decision in your life. That's not just hours lost - that's years of your life spent waiting in metaphorical airports because you never learned to price risk correctly.

Most people are time-poor not because they lack time, but because they waste it trying to buy certainty in a world that doesn't sell it. They confuse abundance of buffer with intelligence of calculation. They think more margin equals more safety, when often it just equals more waste.

The truly valuable skill isn't in maximizing your safety buffer - it's in precisely calculating how much buffer you actually need. This extends far beyond airports. It's about knowing exactly how much market research you need before launching. Understanding exactly how much capital you need to reserve. Calculating exactly how much relationship investment is required for the outcome you want.

When I made my flight on Monday with fifteen minutes to spare, it wasn't luck. It was math. It was knowledge. It was precision. The 3-hour-early crowd was still sitting there, congratulating themselves on their responsible planning while burning hours they'll never get back.

You don't win by hiding from risk under piles of buffer. You win by learning to price it correctly. Master this, and you'll find yourself with years of life that others traded for imaginary security.

That's the real edge. The rest is just waiting in airports.
