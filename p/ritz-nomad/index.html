ritz nomad

June 14, 2026

I'm at the Ritz NoMad last week and the Spurs are staying there too. I walk outside in the morning on the day of the game and there's a decent size crowd of fans and photographers and reporters, immediately I hear a bunch of questions and camera shutters going off and I'm so confused, one guy taps me abruptly on the shoulder as I pass the security rope and then I realize I'm dressed in all black and people think I'm with the organization.

I thought it was funny because later that day I'm in the elevator with what I think was the s&c coach, not paying attention, and I get off on the residents club floor with him by accident. Doors open and it's the entire team. Nobody questions me for a second because I kind of look the part. Wemby looks like an actual alien in person.

But all of this is besides the point, the idea that led me to writing this hit me at 2am later that night after the game. I'm sitting out front of the hotel smoking a cigar with my friend Brougkr and across the street there's people in full Knicks attire just standing around staring at the door and talking. Idk if they were waiting to see a player or looking to do something else but this behavior has always baffled me. How could you be so invested in another human besides yourself like this.

2am. The game had been over for hours. They either went home and came back or they never left. Standing across the street in another team's gear, staring at a door, waiting for it to do something. Nothing happened. Nobody came out. Just them and the door.

I've watched people do this my whole life. A guy will give you the full bench rotation of a team on the other side of the country. Who's hurt. Who got robbed in the trade. What the coach blew in the fourth. Then you ask him what he's building with his own life and he goes quiet. His head is full of furniture other people moved in. None of it is his.

There are two kinds of people. People who talk about everybody, and people everybody talks about. That's the whole split. Almost nobody is the second kind and almost everyone spends their entire life trying to stand as close to it as the rope will let them. That's the sidewalk. That's the jersey with another man's name on the back. Getting as physically close as you can to someone who has what you don't and calling it being a fan.

Talking about everybody is the cheapest thing a person can do. It costs nothing. You don't build anything. You don't risk anything. You don't become anything. You live off the people who did and pass it around at dinner and it feels like you were in the room. You're into ball. You follow the season. You've got opinions on men getting paid millions to do the one thing you have never once attempted in your life.

And it isn't only sports. The guy who knows the rotation knows everyone's net worth too. You think he's really doing a hundred a month. No shot, it's all financed, the lease isn't even his. Sit at any dinner long enough and it turns into a forensic breakdown of people who aren't in the room. What so and so sold the company for. What the neighbor overpaid on the house. Who's quietly drowning behind the flex. They can map every other man's money down to the dollar and couldn't tell you what their own runway looks like. Same fan, different jersey. Every ounce of attention out scouting a life that's never going to be theirs.

The energy never comes back to you either. You get one pool of attention for the whole ride and it's maybe the only thing you actually own. And you hand it out for free to a team, a celebrity, two strangers feuding online who would step over your body in the street. It walks out the door at 2am and parks itself on a curb waiting for someone to appear. Every hour you spend living inside another person's life is an hour your own gets nothing. Every season you follow is a season you didn't have. Every take you sharpen is one nobody's ever going to pay you for.

And nobody chooses this, which is the part that should bother you. You don't sit down one day and decide to spend your life as an audience member. It happens by accretion. You follow one team. Then five. Then you know every storyline in the league. Then it's sports radio every morning in the car. Then your whole mood for the week is riding on whether a man you've never met covered a number in a city you've never been to. Five years go like that. Then ten. Then twenty. You wake up and every strong opinion you own is about a stranger, and your entire inner life got leased out to people who will never hand you a cent of it back. And you called it a hobby.

The Knicks guys were still out there when I went up. Door never opened. Nobody came. And the sidewalk isn't the only place they do this. Whatever was driving them to stand there at 2am is running everything else too, the way they watch instead of move, wait instead of build, live off other people's lives in every corner of their own. You get one reservoir. You can spend it watching other people live, or you can spend it becoming someone worth watching. There was never a third option.
