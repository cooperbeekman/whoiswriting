The Cheetah's Mistake

December 1, 2024

​

Been thinking about cheetahs lately.

Not because they're the fastest land animal - though that's pretty fucking cool. But because they perfectly demonstrate why being the fastest means absolutely nothing if you can't keep what you catch.

Let me explain.

A cheetah hits 70 mph in seconds. Insane acceleration. Unmatched speed. Peak performance that would make a Bugatti jealous. But after that explosive sprint, they're so gassed they physically can't defend their kill.

And then it happens.

They lay there, heaving for breath, watching hyenas, lions, and even vultures casually stroll up and take their prize. All that speed, all that effort, all that energy – wasted. They end up hungry while the "slower" animals feast.

This is the most concentrated form of what I see happening everywhere in business and life. The sprinters. The speed demons. The "first-to-market" obsessed players who end up watching others walk away with their success.

The cheetah's mistake isn't being fast. The mistake is only having speed in a game that demands so much more.

I see these human cheetahs everywhere:

  • Startups sprinting to market with zero infrastructure
  • Traders hitting quick gains then blowing up their accounts
  • Entrepreneurs scaling before their foundation exists

Everyone wants to be a cheetah. Nobody wants to be a lion.

Here's the truth about lions – they don't hit 70 mph. But they keep what they kill. They play a longer, smarter game. They have systems. They have stamina. They understand the deep game.

This shit gets real when you look at actual success: Amazon wasn't the first online bookstore. Facebook wasn't the first social network. Apple wasn't the first to make smartphones.

They weren't cheetahs. They were lions. They built systems that could not just catch success, but keep it. Defend it. Grow it.

Let me say this clearly: Speed without stamina is just fancy failure.

The real edge isn't in the sprint. It's in the stamina. The systems. The ability to not just catch but keep.

Look at most people's pattern:

Sprint to build something first

Exhaust every resource getting there

Nothing left for what comes next

Watch others walk away with their prize

Fuck that.

The game isn't won in the sprint. It's won in the recovery, the sustainability, the ability to go again and again and again.

I credit where I am today to understanding this early. To building systems instead of just speed. To playing the lion's game in a world obsessed with cheetahs.

This is about more than just pace. It's about power. Real power. The kind that lasts.

Most people never figure this out. They'll keep sprinting, keep gasping, keep watching others feast on their kills.

But you're reading this newsletter for a reason.

You're looking for the deeper game. The real edge. The sustainable power.

Stop being the fastest. Start being the last one standing.

That's the edge.

And that's the game.
