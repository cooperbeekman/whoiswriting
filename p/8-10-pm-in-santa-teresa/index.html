10 thoughts from the past few months

  1. The feeling of being behind is almost always a story problem, not a timing problem. You're not actually behind anyone. You're comparing your interior to someone else's edited exterior and calling the gap a deficit. The cure isn't catching up. The cure is noticing you were running in a race no one else even knew was happening.
  2. Most of what people call discipline is just the absence of attractive alternatives. Monks aren't disciplined. They live in a place with nothing else to do. Build the environment and the discipline shows up on its own. Trying to white knuckle willpower in a room engineered to break it is the dumbest possible way to fight that war.
  3. Your taste is the only real moat you have. Skills get cloned, capital gets matched, networks get rebuilt. Taste is the one thing that took twenty years of specific exposure to develop and can't be reverse engineered by someone who didn't live your life. Everyone's racing to acquire skills the market values. Almost no one is sharpening the one thing that's actually theirs.
  4. The scariest thing about getting what you want is finding out it didn't work. As long as the goal is out there, you get to keep the fantasy that achieving it solves the underlying thing. Most people sandbag right at the finish line because the alternative is realizing the target was never the problem. The cope of the chase is more comfortable than the silence of arrival.
  5. Boredom is a signal you've stopped growing, not that the situation is stale. Same job, same gym, same city, same partner. The boredom isn't coming from outside. It's coming from you running the same loop without going deeper. The novelty seekers are always restarting. The deep ones stay and find another floor underneath the floor.
  6. Patience is the most underpriced asset in the world right now. Everyone is sized for instant payoff, so anything that takes three to five years to mature has almost no competition. The whole game has been arbitraged down to the next twelve weeks. If you can hold a position, a project, a relationship, a thesis, longer than the median attention span, you basically have a free edge that compounds while everyone else churns themselves into the ground.
  7. The body you have at 35 is a portrait of what you tolerated at 25. Same goes for the bank account, the friend group, the marriage. The present is a slow developing photograph of decisions made when the stakes felt theoretical. Whatever you're tolerating right now is what your future self will be living inside. The frame is being painted in real time and you're holding the brush.
  8. Status games eat the people who win them faster than the ones who lose. Losing one frees you to opt out. Winning one ties your identity to the leaderboard, which means every move forward now requires defending what you have instead of building what you want. The trap of the prize is that it makes you smaller than you were when you were chasing it.
  9. Almost every problem in your life would dissolve if you could just sit with one uncomfortable feeling for ten minutes without trying to fix it. The whole apparatus of modern life is built to prevent that ten minutes from ever happening. Phone, food, scroll, drink, scroll, work, scroll. The feeling never gets felt, so the underlying thing never gets metabolized, so it leaks into every decision you make for the rest of your life. Ten minutes of sitting with it would unlock more than any framework, podcast, or book ever will.
  10. The work that actually moves your life forward is almost never the work that feels productive. Deep thinking looks like staring at a wall. Real recovery looks like doing nothing. Strategy looks like wasted afternoons. The stuff that registers as productive is mostly motion designed to perform productivity to yourself. The highest leverage hours of your week probably look completely useless from the outside, which is exactly why almost no one logs them.
