You Don't Learn How to Drive in a Parked Car

December 18, 2024

​

Had an interesting conversation with a young guy last week who's been "preparing" to start his business for the past two years. Reading every book. Taking every course. Building the perfect plan. Making sure everything's perfect before he starts.

It reminded me of when my dad taught me to drive.

No instructor. No manual. No preparation. He just threw me in the passenger seat, looked at me and said "start driving." Everything I thought I knew about driving from watching him all those years meant absolutely nothing the moment that car started moving under my control. The actual feeling of a car responding to your inputs is a reality that nothing can prepare you for.

This is what most people don't understand about success, business, and life in general. They think they can learn to drive while the car is parked. They think they can master the ocean by reading about waves. They think they can become great by studying greatness.

But motion creates clarity. Movement reveals reality. Action exposes truth.

Most people are sitting in parked cars, engines off, reading manuals about driving. They're mastering theory while missing reality. They're preparing for perfection while avoiding progress. They're learning about the game instead of playing it.

I see this pattern everywhere. The wannabe entrepreneur reading about business instead of starting one. The aspiring investor paper trading instead of putting skin in the game. The future creator planning content instead of publishing it. My dad didn't hand me a driving manual - he handed me keys. That's the difference.

When you're actually driving, the road gives you feedback. Every turn, every brake, every acceleration teaches you something that no manual could explain. The car talks to you. The road educates you. Reality mentors you. Just like that first drive with my dad - the instant feedback of a car moving under your control teaches you more than months of theory ever could.

The same is true in business and life. Every customer interaction teaches you more than a hundred books on customer service. Every failed product tells you more than a thousand market research reports. Every real investment teaches you more than years of paper trading.

Motion is the ultimate teacher. My dad understood this. He knew no amount of preparation could replace the raw education of actually doing it.

This isn't about being reckless. It's not about driving without any knowledge or safety considerations. But there's a point where more preparation becomes procrastination in disguise. Where more planning becomes a sophisticated form of fear. Where more research becomes resistance to reality.

The young guy I talked to? He knows more about business theory than most successful entrepreneurs. He can quote every business book. Recite every startup principle. Explain every business model.

But he hasn't made a single dollar. Hasn't talked to a single customer. Hasn't faced a single real-world problem.

He's sitting in a parked car, engine off, reading about driving.

The truth is, you'll learn more in your first month of actual business than in two years of preparation. You'll learn more from your first ten customers than from a hundred customer service books. You'll learn more from your first failure than from a thousand success stories.

Because reality is the ultimate mentor. Action is the ultimate teacher. Motion is the ultimate revealer of truth.

The lesson my dad taught me wasn't just about driving. It was about life. About business. About success. Sometimes you need someone to look at you and say "start driving." Sometimes you need to be thrown into the deep end. Sometimes the only way to learn is to move.

Want to learn business? Start one. Want to learn investing? Invest. Want to learn creation? Create.

Turn the key. Start the engine. Put it in drive.

Because you don't learn how to drive in a parked car. And you don't learn how to succeed by preparing to succeed.

That's not theory. That's reality. That's the edge.
